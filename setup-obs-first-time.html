How to Set Up OBS
for the First Time

OBS looks complicated the first time you open it. It isn't. Here's the exact setup process, step by step, so you can go live today.

OBS Studio (that stands for Open Broadcaster Software) is the program most streamers use to capture their game, their face cam, and their mic, and send all of it out to Twitch or YouTube. It's completely free, it works on Windows, Mac, and Linux, and honestly, most of it you'll never even need to touch. But that first-time setup screen full of boxes and sliders can be intimidating. Let's fix that.

Before You Open OBS

Go to obsproject.com and download the version for your operating system. It's a normal install — just click next a few times like any other program. That's it. No account needed, no credit card, nothing to sign up for. Once it's installed, open it up and you'll probably see a popup asking about "Auto-Configuration." Say yes to that one — it does a lot of the hard work for you.

5 Steps to Your First Working Setup

1

Run the Auto-Configuration Wizard

If OBS didn't already prompt you, find it under Tools → Auto-Configuration Wizard. Tell it whether you're recording, streaming, or both, and it will test your computer and pick a resolution and bitrate that should run smoothly. This alone solves most of what people get stuck on.

2

Add a Display or Game Capture Source

Down in the "Sources" box, click the plus sign and choose "Display Capture" (shows your whole screen) or "Game Capture" (grabs just the game, which usually runs a bit smoother). This is what actually shows up on your stream — without a source added, your viewers just see black.

3

Add Your Webcam

Click the plus sign again and pick "Video Capture Device." Choose your webcam from the list, then drag the corner of the box to resize it and drop it wherever you want your face cam to sit — bottom corner is the classic spot.

4

Set Up Your Audio

Look at the "Audio Mixer" panel at the bottom. You should see a bar for your Mic and one for your Desktop Audio. Talk into your mic — if the bar moves, it's working. If it's dead silent, click the gear icon next to it and check you picked the right microphone in the dropdown.

5

Connect Your Stream Key

Go to Settings → Stream, pick Twitch or YouTube from the service list, and either log in or paste in your stream key from your account's dashboard. This is the last step — once this is filled in, the "Start Streaming" button actually works.

⚠️ Most Common First-Timer Mistake

Forgetting to select the right microphone, so the stream goes out completely silent. Always do a quick 10-second test recording before your first real stream and actually watch it back with sound on.

Quick Checklist Before You Go Live

  • Ran the Auto-Configuration Wizard
  • Added a Display or Game Capture source
  • Added and positioned your webcam
  • Confirmed the mic bar moves when you talk
  • Pasted in your stream key under Settings → Stream
  • Recorded a 10-second test clip and watched it back
💡 Don't Overthink It

You do not need perfect settings on day one. Default resolution and the Auto-Configuration Wizard's suggestions are good enough to start. You can always come back and tweak bitrate, scenes, and overlays once you're comfortable — most streamers change their setup constantly as they go.

What to Do Right After Setup

Do one full test stream before you tell anyone to watch. Set it to "unlisted" or just stream to an empty room for ten minutes. Talk the whole time like someone's actually watching. Check that your audio, video, and webcam all showed up the way you expected. Fixing an awkward mic placement or a game that's not capturing properly is a lot less stressful when nobody's there watching it happen.

Once that test goes smoothly, you're genuinely ready. The setup is the boring part — the good news is you only really have to do it once.
