How to Fix Dropped Frames
& Lag in OBS

Stuttering stream? Viewers saying it's buffering? Here's exactly what's wrong and how to fix it — in the order you should try.

By the StreamStarters Team · Last updated July 22, 2026

Few things are more frustrating than finally going live and having your stream stutter, freeze, or buffer. The good news: 90% of OBS lag problems come down to a handful of causes, and most of them take under two minutes to fix. Let's go through them in the order most likely to solve your problem.

First: Figure Out Which Problem You Have

OBS tells you what's wrong if you know where to look. While streaming, check the bottom-right corner of OBS. If you see "Dropped frames (network)" climbing, your internet is the problem. If OBS warns "Encoding overloaded!", your computer is the problem. These have completely different fixes, so check this first before changing anything.

Fixes for Dropped Frames (Internet Problems)

1

Lower Your Bitrate

Go to Settings → Output and check your video bitrate. The golden rule: your bitrate should be no more than 70% of your actual upload speed. Test your real speed at speedtest.net — if you upload at 6 Mbps (6000 Kbps), stream at 4000 Kbps max. For most beginners, 3500–4500 Kbps at 720p is the sweet spot.

2

Use Ethernet Instead of Wi-Fi

This is the single biggest stability upgrade available, and the cable costs a few dollars. Wi-Fi speed bounces around constantly — a wired connection doesn't. If you can't run a cable, at least move closer to your router and get other devices off the network while you stream.

3

Change Your Ingest Server

OBS sends your stream to a Twitch server, and sometimes the auto-selected one is having a bad day. In Settings → Stream, change the server from "Auto" to a specific server near your location. Try a couple — the difference can be dramatic.

Fixes for Encoding Lag (Computer Problems)

1

Switch Your Encoder to Hardware

In Settings → Output, look at "Encoder." If it's set to x264 (software), your CPU is doing all the work while also running your game. If you have an NVIDIA graphics card, switch to NVENC; on AMD, use the AMF/HW encoder; on newer Intel chips, QuickSync. Hardware encoding takes the load off your CPU almost entirely.

2

Lower Your Output Resolution

Streaming at 1080p sounds nice, but 720p at a smooth frame rate looks far better to viewers than 1080p that stutters. In Settings → Video, set your Output (Scaled) Resolution to 1280x720. This is what most small streamers should be using anyway.

3

Cap Your Game's FPS

If your game runs uncapped at 300fps, it's fighting OBS for resources. Cap the game at 60 or 120fps in its own settings. Your game will feel identical, and OBS suddenly has room to breathe.

⚠️ Don't Do This

Don't randomly copy "pro streamer settings" from YouTube videos. Their settings are built for their hardware and internet — not yours. A 8000 Kbps bitrate on a connection that can't handle it is exactly how you get dropped frames in the first place.

The 2-Minute Pre-Stream Check

  • Run a speed test — is your upload at least 1.5x your bitrate?
  • Close Chrome tabs, Discord video, and anything else uploading data
  • Make sure no one else on your network is streaming Netflix or gaming online
  • Do a 2-minute test stream and watch the dropped frames counter

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my stream lagging but my game isn't?

That's the classic sign of a network problem, not a PC problem. Your game runs locally, but your stream depends on upload speed. Lower your bitrate and switch to a wired connection.

What bitrate should I use for Twitch?

3500–4500 Kbps for 720p is right for most beginners. Never set your bitrate higher than about 70% of your tested upload speed.

Does a better PC fix dropped frames?

No — dropped frames are a network issue. A better PC fixes encoding lag. Check which warning OBS is actually giving you before spending money.
